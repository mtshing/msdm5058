1. Sequential Game¶

So far our discussion has been assuming the same game is played once only and we are looking at the players' best strategies in this single round. However more often we need to play a game for multiple rounds, and the payoff at each round are possibly different and depends on the strategies used in previous rounds. Thus we need some more specific concepts to describe the equilibrium of such games.

1.1. Subgame perfect equilibrium¶

Although being a fancy name, a subgame perfect equilibrium is, informaly, the best outcome arrived after players predicting the best action his opponent can choose in the subsequent rounds. To understand it better, we can first visualize a sequential game via a decision tree, for example:

Literally, a "subgame" is a part of the sequential game, which when isolated, constitute a game itself. In the decision tree, each subgame can be identified as each node with all of its descendent branches - a game can be started at the node without knowing the actions of the players before the node. In this example we can identify subgames of three different levels:

  • $(A_1, B_1)$, $(C_1, D_1)$, $(E_1, F_1, G_1)$ = subgames of 1st level (green)
  • $(a_1, b_1, c_1)$, $(d_1, e_1)$ = subgames of 2nd level (red)
  • $(A_3, B_3)$ = subgame of 3rd level (blue)

Then we can formally define the subgame perfect equilibrium of a game as the strategy profile which points to the Nash equilibrium in all of its subgames. If the game only consists of finite steps, the equilibrium is commonly searched by backward induction to the player's best payoff in each of the subgame. Demonstrating for the above example:

  • 1st level subgames = player 1's turn = look at player 1's payoff
    • $(A_1, B_1)$: Choose $A_1$ for payoff $= (\mathbf{6},1)$.
    • $(C_1, D_1)$: Choose $D_1$ for payoff $= (\mathbf{5},4)$.
    • $(E_1, F_1, G_1)$: Choose $G_1$ for payoff $= (\mathbf{3},4)$.
  • 2nd level subgames = player 2's turn = look at player 2's payoff
    • $(a_1, b_1, c_1)$: Choose $c_2$ for payoff $= (5, \mathbf{4})$.
    • $(d_1, e_1)$: Choose $d_2$ for payoff $= (3,\mathbf{4})$.
  • 3rd level subgames = player 1's turn = look at player 1's payoff
    • $(A_3, B_3)$: Choose $A_3$ for payoff $= (\mathbf{5},4)$.

Therefore $A_3\rightarrow c_2\rightarrow D_1$ is the subgame perfect equilibrium of this game with payoff $=(5,4)$.

Note that in order to determine the sub-game equilibrium, a player will need perfect information about his opponents' payoff at each of the outcome on the decision tree.

1.2 Repeated game¶

In the case when players have the same set of strategies with equal payoff increment every round, the sequential game is identified as a repeated game, and the game of the repeating payoff matrix is called the stage game. Depends on how many rounds the stage game is played, it is further separated into

  • Finitely repeated game - if the game ends after certain rounds of play. It is possible to determine the subgame perfect equilibrium by backward induction.

  • Infinitely repeated game - if the game never ends, or if the players do not know when the game will end. Not possible to use backward induction as there is no "last round".

Depends on if the game is finitely or infinitely played, the optimal strategy can be very different.

1.2.1. Folk theorems¶

The Folk theorems is a group of theorems about the results of optimal strategies in repeated games. They are named "Folk" because these results are somehow intuitional and well-known among game theorists in 1950s although nobody formally published them.

  • Finitely repeated game - If the stage game has a Nash equilibrium, we can simply deduce by backward induction that players will always follow their Nash equilibrium strategy.

  • Infinitely repeated game - Players should instead cooperate and play any social optimal strategy, i.e. any strategy profile better than the Nash equilibrium (if there are any). The main difference from finite version is that, if at any round a player decides to deviate from the social optimal for its own selfishness, his opponent can always punish him at future rounds by using a strategy that reduce his payoff. This also gives more aspects in modeling the game, e.g.

    • Magnitude of punishment - Do punishments exist? Is the reduction in payoff enough to stop the player deviating from the social optimal?
    • Sensitivity to punishment - How will the player react depends on the magnitude of punishment?

1.2.2. Discounting¶

The discount factor $0\leq\delta\leq 1$ is a parameter modeling how a player value the payoff of the game depends on the number of rounds, or more commonly, describing how "patient" the player is.

  • $\delta=1$ - The player is totally patient. He views any future payoff equivalent to the payoff at current round.
  • $\delta=0$ - The player is totally impatient. He only cares the payoff at the first round and disregards any future payoff.

Discounting is commonly used to account for the deviation of strategy and review the effectiveness of the punishment. Let's say if the payoff at each round is $\pi$, by geometrical sum the player's total payoff $\Pi$ as

$$ \Pi = \pi + \delta\pi + \delta^2\pi + ... = \frac{1}{1-\delta}\pi $$

We can check if the player has incentive to deviate his strategy with some simple math. Assume the player deviates at the k$^\text{th}$ round, which makes his payoff increase from $\pi$ to $\pi'$. Then his payoff will become

\begin{align*} \Pi' &= \pi + \delta\pi + \delta^2\pi + ... + \delta^{k-1}\pi'+ \delta^k \pi' \\ &= \frac{1-\delta^{k-1}}{1-\delta}\pi + \frac{\delta^{k-1}}{1-\delta}\pi' \\ &= \Pi + \frac{\delta^{k-1}}{1-\delta}(\pi'-\pi) \end{align*}

So we can see that

  • If a player wants to deviate his strategy, deviating at the 1st round will maximize his payoff.
  • If his opponent wants to punish his deviation of strategy, the reduction in payoff needs to be at least $\dfrac{1}{1-\delta}(\pi'-\pi)$.

The solutions dealing with discounting are usually considered as parts of the folk theorems, together with more variations. You may check the Wiki) page for more details.

2. Evolutionary game¶

The "survival of the fittest" is a well-known phrase from evolution theory in biology. One can apply this idea into game theory - we believe that when a game is played infinitely, better strategies will eventually evolve and be played by majority of the players.

2.1. Evolutionary stable strategy¶

Here we introduce the concept of evolutionary stable strategy (ESS), which is a strategy stablely played by majority of players even when a small portion of players deviate their strategies. We can consider a group of players, which a game is played whenever two of the players meet. For simplicity, we can assume the game is symmetric, i.e. all players have the same set of strategies $(q_1,q_2,...)$ and the same payoff function $\pi_1(q_i, q_j)=\pi_2(q_i,q_j)$. The strategies can be identified into two kinds:

  • The usual strategy $q^*$, which is the strategy played by the majority of the players.
  • Other deviant strategies $q^D_1, q^D_2, ...$, which are the strategies players can deviate into.

Let $p_1, p_2,...$ be the probability of a player playing the deviant strategy $q^D_1, q^D_2,...$, which are very small number such that $1 \gg p_j >0$. We can define the fitness of a strategy $F(q)$ as the expected payoff when it is played by one of the player (let's say player 1):

  • Fitness of the usual strategy $q^*$: $$ F(q^*) = \underbrace{\pi(q^*,q^*)}_{\substack{\text{payoff when}\\ \text{player 2}\\ \text{plays }q^*}}\times \underbrace{\left(1-\sum_i p_i\right)}_{\substack{\text{prob. of player 2}\\ \text{playing }q^*}} + \sum_i \underbrace{\pi(q^*,q^D_i)}_{\substack{\text{payoff when}\\ \text{player 2}\\ \text{plays }q^D_i}} \times\underbrace{p_i}_{\substack{\text{prob. of}\\ \text{ player 2}\\\text{playing }q^D_i}} $$

  • Fitness of the j$^\text{th}$ deviant strategy $q^D_j$: $$ F(q^D_j) = \underbrace{\pi(q^D_j,q^*)}_{\substack{\text{payoff when}\\ \text{player 2}\\ \text{plays }q^*}}\times \underbrace{\left(1-\sum_i p_i\right)}_{\substack{\text{prob. of player 2}\\ \text{playing }q^*}} + \sum_i \underbrace{\pi(q^D_j,q^D_i)}_{\substack{\text{payoff when}\\ \text{player 2}\\ \text{plays }q^D_i}} \times\underbrace{p_i}_{\substack{\text{prob. of}\\ \text{ player 2}\\\text{playing }q^D_i}} $$

We can say that the usual strategy is fitter than the deviant strategies $q^D_j$ if $F(q^*) > F(q^D_j)$. By subtraction,

$$ F(q^*) - F(q^D_j) = \left[\pi(q^*,q^*)-\pi(q^D_j,q^*)\right]\left(1-\sum_i p_i\right) + \sum_i\left[\pi(q^*, q^D_i)-\pi(q^D_j, q^D_i)\right]p_i >0 \\ $$$$ \Rightarrow \left[\pi(q^*,q^*)-\pi(q^D_j,q^*)\right] + \sum_i\left[\pi(q^*, q^D_i)-\pi(q^D_j, q^D_i)\right]\frac{p_i}{1-\sum_i p_i} >0 $$

Since $\frac{p_i}{1-\sum_i p_i}$ should be a very small number, we can assume the magnitude of the first term being much greater than the second term. So for the inequality to hold, we can either have:

  1. $\pi(q^*,q^*)-\pi(q^D_j,q^*) >0$. i.e. The usual strategy is a strict Nash equilibrium. Playing the usual strategy is always better off than playing any deviant strategies.

  2. $\pi(q^*,q^*)-\pi(q^D_j,q^*) =0 \ $ and $\ \pi(q^*, q^D_i)-\pi(q^D_j, q^D_i) >0$, i.e. although playing the usual strategy is not advantegous against another player using usual strategy, it is still advantegous against a player using any deviant strategies.

For any strategy $q^*$ that satisfies any one of these two conditions, it is a evolutionary stable strategy (ESS). We can also easily deduce that whenever the strategy $q^*$ is an ESS, $(q^*, q^*)$ is also a Nash equilibrium (However not in the opposite case).

Example 1: Prisoner's dilemma¶

Consider a modified version of prisoner's dilemma as below. Analyze if there are any ESSs.

$$ (\mathbf{A},\mathbf{A}^\intercal) = \left(\begin{array}{c|ccc} & \text{Confess} & \text{Deny}\\ \hline \text{Confess} & (1,1) & (6,1) \\ \text{Deny} & (1,6) & (4,4) \end{array}\right) $$

Solution. First of all, there are 3 pure Nash equilibrium and no mixed Nash equilibrium.

$$ \left(\begin{array}{c|ccc} & \text{Confess} & \text{Deny}\\ \hline \text{Confess} & (\boxed{1},\boxed{1}) & (\boxed{6},\boxed{1}) \\ \text{Deny} & (\boxed{1},\boxed{6}) & (4,4) \end{array}\right) $$

Since none of them are strict Nash equilibrium, the first condition of ESS fails. For the second condition, consider the payoff of the first player, we can see that

$$ \begin{cases} \pi(\text{Confess},\text{Confess}) - \pi(\text{Deny},\text{Confess}) = 1-1 = 0 \\ \pi(\text{Confess},\text{Deny}) - \pi(\text{Deny},\text{Deny}) = 6-4 > 0 \end{cases} $$

Therefore confess is the only ESS in this game.

Example 2: Non-zero sum Rock-Paper_Scissors¶

Consider a modified version of rock-paper-scissors as below, which players have better payoff when there is a draw. Analyze if there are any ESSs.

$$ (\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{A}^\intercal) = \left(\begin{array}{c|cccc} & \text{Rock} & \text{Paper} & \text{Scissors}\\ \hline \text{Rock} & (\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2}) & (-1,1) & (1,-1) \\ \text{Paper} & (1,-1) & (\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2}) & (-1,1) \\ \text{Scissors} & (-1,1) &(1,-1) & (\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2}) \end{array}\right) $$

Solution. There is only one mixed Nash equilibrium $\mathbf{x}^*=(x_1^*, x_2^*, x_3^*) = \left(\frac{1}{3}, \frac{1}{3},\frac{1}{3}\right)$ with the game value $\pi(\mathbf{x}^*,\mathbf{x}^*)= \mathbf{x}^{*\intercal}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}^* = \frac{1}{6}$. Let the deviant strategy be $\mathbf{x}^D=(x^D_1, x^D_2, x^D_3)$, we can compute

\begin{align*} \pi(\mathbf{x}^D,\mathbf{x}^*) &= \pi(\mathbf{x}^*,\mathbf{x}^D)= \mathbf{x}^{*\intercal}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}^D \\ &= \frac{x_1^D + x_2^D + x_3^D}{6} \\ &= \frac{1}{6}\\[1em] % \pi(\mathbf{x}^D,\mathbf{x}^D) &= \mathbf{x}^{D\intercal}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}^D \\ &= \frac{(x_1^D)^2+(x_2^D)^2+(x_3^D)^2}{2} \end{align*}

The first condition of ESS obviously failed. For the second condition we can easily find a $\mathbf{x}^D$, e.g. $(x^D_1, x^D_2, x^D_3)=(1,0,0)$ such that $\frac{(x_1^D)^2+(x_2^D)^2+(x_3^D)^2}{2}=\frac{1}{2}>\frac{1}{6}$. Therefore $\left(\frac{1}{3}, \frac{1}{3},\frac{1}{3}\right)$ is not a ESS and this game has no ESS.

2.2 Replicator equation¶

We can further generalize the idea of ESS to examine the evolutions of usage of each strategy in the population. Now consider there is no more a usual strategy used by the majority, but each strategy are initially used by a significant portion of the players. Let each of the strategy $(q_1, q_2,...)$ is played by a portion $(p_1, p_2,...)$ of players, the fitness of a strategy $q_i$ can be calculated as its expected payoff:

$$ F(q_i) = \sum_j \pi(q_i, q_j)p_j $$

Then the overall fitness (average payoff) of all the players is

$$ F = \sum_i F(q_i)\times p_i = \sum_i\sum_j \pi(q_i, q_j)p_ip_j $$

Now we can look into the evolution of the portion of players. Suppose each portion $(p_1, p_2,...) = (p_1(t), p_2(t),...)$ is a function of time, then we can model the change in the portion into a set of differential equations. One of the way is relating the rate of change of the portion to the difference between the payoff of using strategy $q_j$ and the average payoff of all players, i.e.

\begin{align*} \frac{dp_k(t)}{dt} &= p_k(t)\left[\sum_j \pi(q_k, q_j)p_j(t) - \sum_i\sum_j \pi(q_i, q_j)p_i(t)p_j(t)\right]\\ &\sim \begin{pmatrix}\text{portion of players}\\\text{using strategy }k \end{pmatrix}\times \left[\begin{pmatrix}\text{expected payoff}\\\text{by using strategy }j\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}\text{average payoff}\\\text{among all players}\end{pmatrix} \right] \end{align*}

Such set of equations is also known as replicator equation in game theory. Since the equations are generally non-linear, analytical solution is hard to compute and are left to stability analysis. People are mostly interested in its steady state solution, i.e. any solutions that is constant in time in the limit of $t\to \infty$, via taking $\frac{dp_k(t)}{dt}=0$.

\begin{align*} &p_k(t)\left[\sum_j \pi(q_k, q_j)p_j(t) - \sum_i\sum_j \pi(q_i, q_j)p_i(t)p_j(t)\right] = 0 \\[0.5em] &\text{with } \sum_k p_k(t) = 1 \text{ and } 0 \leq p_k(t)\leq 1 \end{align*}

With a little algebra and theories of differential equations, one can also show that the set of equations always have unique solution given that the initial portions of players are provided, and any asymtotcially stable solutions are the ESS of the game.

Example: Prisoner's dilemma again¶

Consider the same game matrix as the previous example. This time analyze the stability of the games using replicator dynamics.

$$ (\mathbf{A},\mathbf{A}^\intercal) = \left(\begin{array}{c|ccc} & \text{Confess} & \text{Deny}\\ \hline \text{Confess} & (1,1) & (6,1) \\ \text{Deny} & (1,6) & (4,4) \end{array}\right) $$

Solution. The overall expected payoff is

$$ \sum_{i=1,2}\sum_{j=1,2} A_{ij}p_ip_j = p_1^2 + 7p_1p_2+4p_2^2 $$

The replication equations at time limit $t\to\infty$ write as follow:

$$ \begin{cases} \dfrac{dp_1}{dt} = p_1\left[p_1+6p_2- (p_1^2 + 7p_1p_2+4p_2^2)\right] =0\\[0.5em] \dfrac{dp_2}{dt} = p_2\left[p_1+4p_2- (p_1^2 + 7p_1p_2+4p_2^2)\right] =0\\[0.5em] \text{with } p_1+p_2=1 \end{cases} $$

which you should find the 2 valid solutions $(p_1, p_2)=(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$, corresponding to purely confess or purely deny. We can further grpah out $p_1$ vs $p_2$ as direction field $f(p_1,p_2) = \left(\frac{dp_1}{dt}, \frac{dp_2}{dt}\right)$ for studying the equations' stability:

As we can see from the arrows' flows, when there are any players choose to confess during the game ($p_1>0$), the solution will eventaully converge to $(p_1,p_2)=(1,0)$, i.e. leading to the whole population choose to confess. This also indicates that $(p_1,p_2) = (1,0)$ is an evolutionarily stable solution. The only exception is that if all players choose to deny, which can keep the player's portion to remain at $(p_1,p_2)=(0,1)$ until any player betrays.
